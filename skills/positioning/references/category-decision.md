# The Category Decision

The most consequential choice in the plan. Everything downstream — buyers,
competitors, pricing power, demand engines — follows from it.

## Generate 2–3 candidates

Start from the Market Map: existing categories in play, `[GAP]` positions,
and emerging language buyers already use. A candidate can be:
- **An existing category** the company can win a defensible corner of.
- **A subcategory** ("X for [vertical/segment]") — often the sweet spot for
  smaller B2B companies.
- **A new category** — highest upside, highest education cost. Viable only
  with evidence, patience and share of voice; be honest about all three.

## Evaluate each candidate on four criteria

1. **Size of the space** — are there enough buyers who look for solutions
   using this frame?
2. **Saturation** — how many credible voices already claim it (per the Map)?
3. **Capability fit** — can this company back the category claim with what it
   actually does well today?
4. **ICP appeal** — does the frame match how the decision-maker already
   thinks about the problem?

Score honestly. A category that excites the founder but fails criterion 4
will produce a plan that markets to a mirror.

## Decide and state implications

Choosing a category means choosing: who you compete against, who you sell
to, what alternatives you declare obsolete, and what price frame you enter.
Write these implications down explicitly — they constrain every later step.
