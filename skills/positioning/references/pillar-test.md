# The Pillar Test

A pillar is a reason to believe the promise. Every candidate pillar passes
all three checks or it is cut.

## 1. Relevant — does the ICP care?
The pillar addresses something the decision-maker actually weighs. Test:
would it survive in a competitive deal conversation, or is it something only
the company finds interesting about itself?

## 2. Differentiated — could a competitor say the same?
Check against the Market Map. If the two nearest competitors could plausibly
put the same sentence on their homepage, it is table stakes, not a pillar.
Table stakes can be mentioned; they cannot carry the positioning.

## 3. Defensible — can we prove it today?
The pillar is backed by current, demonstrable capabilities: named cases,
data, referenceable customers, observable mechanics. If evidence is missing,
either downgrade the pillar or keep it flagged `unverified-claim` with a
plan to obtain proof. A pillar built on aspiration collapses in the first
serious sales conversation.

## Format per pillar

- **Name** — short, actionable.
- **What it claims** — one clear sentence.
- **Why it is credible** — the capability behind it.
- **Why it is differential** — against whom, specifically.
- **Evidence** — cases, data, references, or the open question.

## Count

3–4 pillars. With fewer, the promise stands on too little; with more, none
of them is remembered.
