# Execution Readiness

The closing section of the GTM Plan. Its job is honesty about scope: this
plan decides positioning and demand; it does not build the machinery that
converts and operates them. Map what remains, factually.

## Structure — three workstreams

For each workstream: what it is, why it matters, what typically happens when
it is skipped, and what capacity executing it requires.

### 1. Conversion design
The handoff between marketing and sales: qualification criteria and routing,
SLAs, pipeline stages and advancement criteria, the sales playbook.
Skipped → the engines generate demand that leaks between marketing and
sales, and the plan gets blamed for a conversion problem.

### 2. Deployment
The operating layer: content calendars and production, campaign and media
plans, communication and social playbooks, the cadences that keep engines
running weekly.
Skipped → the plan stays a document; engines start and stall on the first
busy month.

### 3. Measurement
Metrics architecture: what is measured, from which source, reviewed by whom,
unblocking which decision; reporting cadence and CRM/data hygiene.
Skipped → by month three nobody can say which engine is working, and budget
follows opinions.

## Capacity note

Close with a factual statement, not a pitch: executing these workstreams
requires either an internal team with revenue-operations experience or a
specialized partner. List the specific capabilities the roadmap assumes
(e.g. content production, paid media operation, CRM administration) so the
user can audit their own gap.

## Tone rules

No sales language. No calls to action. No "we can help". The section is a
map of what remains, written so a sophisticated reader draws their own
conclusions.
