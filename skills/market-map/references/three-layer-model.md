# The Three-Layer Competition Model

Most competitive analyses only look at layer 1. Deals are lost in all three.

## Layer 1 — Direct competitors
Same offer, same ICP. The names the user already knows. Usually 3–10
companies. Analyze the 2–3 most relevant in depth.

## Layer 2 — Category competitors
Different product or service, same job to be done. A marketing agency, a
fractional CMO and a GTM SaaS tool can all compete for the same budget line.
Identify them by asking: *what else solves this buyer's problem, even badly?*

## Layer 3 — Non-competitive alternatives
What the buyer does if nothing in the category convinces them:
- **Hire internally** — headcount instead of vendor.
- **Do it by hand** — spreadsheets, intuition, founder time.
- **Do nothing** — live with the pain. In B2B this is usually the largest
  "competitor" by share of decisions.

## Why layer 3 matters for positioning
Messaging that only differentiates against layer 1 loses every deal decided
at layer 3. If "do nothing" wins most often, the positioning problem is
urgency and category education, not feature superiority — and the demand plan
must reflect that.

## Practical rule
For each layer, one question:
- L1: *Why us and not them?*
- L2: *Why this kind of solution and not that kind?*
- L3: *Why act at all, and why now?*
